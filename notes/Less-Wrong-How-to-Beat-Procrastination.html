Less Wrong: How to Beat Procrastination

written on August 2, 2013

The article is available here.

The Procrastination Equation

Motivation=Expectancy×ValueImpulsiveness×Delay

where Expectancy is the likelihood of success, Value is the value form the task (including enjoyment), Implusiveness is how easy it is to defer the task in favor of other things (personality and environment) and Delay is the amount of time before the value will be realized.

Putting the Equation into Action

Thus to beat procrastination we need to increase the nominator or decrease the denominator.

Increase Expectancy

  • Success spirals: Confidence increases after achieving a number of challenging goals in a row. To start goals that focus on effort rather than outcome might be the way to go.

  • Vicarious victory: Optimism is contagious. Surrond yourself with positive people. Checkout the right groups (Toastmasters, etc.). Watching inspirational movies, reading inspirational biographies and listening to motivational speakers also helps.

  • Mental contrasting: Contrast what you want with what you currently have.

Increase value

It’s hard to be motivated by something that has low value to us or is unpleasant. The good news is that value can be to some degree constructed or relative.

  • Flow: If a job is boring try to make it more difficult to match your current ability. Then you’ll achieve flow. Think of the state troopers in Super Troopers.

  • Meaning: Realize why you want to do something, what it means to you.

  • Energy: Jobs are easier to do when you have the right energy.

  • Rewards: Reward yourself for completion.

  • Make it more pleasant: Find a way to make a task more pleasant. For example, find a workout partner whose company you enjoy.

Handle Impulsiveness

  • Commit now: Ulysses did not make it past the sirens with willpower. Rather, he knew about his weakness and set things up so he couldn’t change his mind when the time came. A good way to do this is are things like beeminder.

  • Set goals: Specifically, its important to set small subgoals. Daily goals are good, but sometimes it can help to set very tiny goals (like for the first 5 minutes) just to get started.

  • Routine: We’re creatures of habit, it helps to get into a routine.

  • Environment: You can’t change yourself, but you can change your environment.
